Tracking Lot Performance with Data

April 2026 · 7 min read

When you're growing a few hundred pounds of mushrooms a year, you can keep most things in your head. You know which strain colonized fast, which bag produced the best second flush, and which shelf in the fruiting chamber consistently underperforms. But as production scales, memory becomes unreliable. You need data.

We started tracking every production lot in a spreadsheet during our first year. That spreadsheet has since evolved into a structured database that feeds our decision-making about strain selection, substrate formulation, and chamber configuration. This article explains what we track, why, and what we've learned.

What We Track Per Lot

Each production lot gets a unique ID at the time of inoculation. That ID follows the mushrooms from grain to harvest to compost. Here's what we record at each stage:

Culture Stage

Master culture source (which slant or master plate), date of transfer to agar, days to full plate colonization, and a qualitative vigor score (1–5). This tells us whether the culture we started with was performing at baseline.

Grain Spawn Stage

Grain type, hydration time, autoclave cycle number, inoculation date, days to full colonization (visual estimate), and contamination check result. We also note any off-odors or unusual coloration during incubation.

Substrate Stage

Substrate recipe and batch number, pasteurization date and temperature, spawn rate (by weight), and bag weight. The bag weight at inoculation is our baseline for calculating yield as a percentage of substrate weight.

Fruiting Stage

Transfer date to fruiting chamber, shelf position, days to first pin, days to first harvest, individual flush weights (grams), and subjective scores for cap color, cluster density, cap size, and stem length. We also record the date the block was removed and its final weight for compost calculations.

Over 18 months, we identified that one specific shelf position consistently underperformed by 15% — the exhaust fan was creating a dry zone we hadn't noticed.

What the Data Has Taught Us

Shelf Position Matters More Than We Thought

After 18 months of tracking, we noticed that one shelf position in our fruiting chamber was consistently 15% below the average yield. The shelf was directly in the path of the exhaust fan's return air stream — a dry microclimate that we hadn't registered subjectively. Adding a baffle to diffuse the airflow brought that shelf back within the normal range.

First Flush Predicts Total Yield

The correlation between first flush weight and total yield across three flushes is r = 0.87 in our dataset. A strong first flush (60% or more of expected total) is a reliable indicator that the block will produce well through subsequent flushes. A weak first flush (below 40%) rarely recovers, even with extended resting periods.

Strain × Substrate Interactions

Our three oyster strains respond differently to substrate variations. Golden Oyster shows a 22% yield improvement on straw-heavy blends compared to sawdust. Black Pearl, conversely, prefers higher cardboard content — presumably because the denser mycelium benefits from the additional lignin and cellulose structure.

Flush Timing Predicts Quality

Clusters that pin within 5–7 days of the fruiting induction consistently score higher on our quality rubric (cap color, cluster density) than clusters that take 10+ days to pin. We now use pin timing as an early quality indicator — if a block hasn't pinned by day 8, we adjust humidity or check for contamination.

Building Your Own Tracking System

You don't need a database to start tracking your production. A spreadsheet with columns for lot ID, strain, inoculation date, substrate type, flush weights, and notes is enough to reveal patterns. The key is consistency — record every lot, every time, in the same format. The patterns will emerge faster than you expect.

We use a simple scoring rubric for subjective traits: cap color (1 = pale/bleached, 5 = deep/vivid), cluster density (1 = sparse, 5 = tight bouquet), stem length (1 = long/stretchy, 5 = short/compact). These scores, averaged across several batches, correlate well with customer feedback and help us make strain selection decisions.

What's Next

We're working on integrating environmental sensor data (CO₂, temperature, humidity at multiple points in the chamber) into our tracking system. The goal is to correlate specific environmental conditions with yield and quality outcomes — and eventually to build a predictive model that tells us the optimal harvest window for each strain.
