Why Straw & Cardboard

A Regenerative Substrate

Local Straw

We source wheat straw from farms within 50 miles of the estate. Straw is a low-impact byproduct of grain harvest — using it for mushroom cultivation gives it a second life before it returns to the soil as compost.

Reclaimed Cardboard

Clean cardboard — often discarded by local businesses — is soaked, shredded, and blended into the substrate mix. Cardboard provides cellulose and lignin that oyster mycelium thrives on, while diverting waste from landfills.

Pasteurization Process

Straw is chopped to 2–4 inch lengths, loaded into mesh bags, and submerged in a hot water bath at 160 °F for 90 minutes. This kills competitor molds while preserving beneficial thermophilic bacteria that help protect the spawn.

Closed-Loop System

After fruiting, spent blocks are broken open and added to our compost piles. The fully colonized straw breaks down rapidly, creating rich compost for the farm's garden soil. Nothing goes to landfill.

Questions

Substrate FAQs

How long will the block stay fresh before use? +
Use within 7–10 days of receipt for best results. Store in a cool (50–60 °F) area out of direct sunlight. If you need to delay, refrigerate — but allow the block to return to room temperature before inoculating.
Do I need to add water before inoculating? +
No — the blocks are hydrated to 65–70% moisture content before shipping. Simply break up the grain spawn, mix it evenly through the straw, and seal the bag. Mist only during the fruiting phase.
Can I use this substrate for species other than oysters? +
Straw and cardboard are ideal for Pleurotus species. Shiitake and Lion's Mane prefer hardwood-based substrates. We recommend our grain spawn page for species-specific guidance.
How many flushes can I expect from one block? +
Typically 2–3 flushes. The first flush is the largest (50–60% of total yield). Soak the block in cold water for 4–6 hours between flushes to replenish moisture.
